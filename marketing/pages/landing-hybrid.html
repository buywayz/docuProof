How it works end-to-end

Under the hood: hashes, OpenTimestamps, Bitcoin and a clean verification surface.

Step 1 · In your browser
Hash is computed locally

You choose a file. The browser computes a SHA-256 hash. The file itself never leaves your machine.

Step 2 · docuProof backend
Proof metadata is stored

docuProof stores the hash, a Proof ID and your display name in Netlify Blobs and prepares your PDF certificate.

Step 3 · OpenTimestamps
Receipt is queued for Bitcoin

A detached OpenTimestamps receipt is created and batched. You can download it and verify independently at any time.

Step 4 · Over time
Anchor + confirmations

When the batch is mined into a Bitcoin transaction, your verify page updates with a txid and confirmations.

Questions you probably have

The full FAQ lives on the start page. Here are the big ones.

Do you ever see or store my file?

No. docuProof computes a SHA-256 hash locally in your browser. Only that hash and your chosen labels (file name, display name, email) are sent to the backend.

Is this a legal notary service?

No. docuProof is a technical proof-of-existence tool, not a notary. It gives you a cryptographic timestamp on your content; your legal position still depends on your jurisdiction and context.

What happens if docuProof disappears?

Your .ots receipt and original file are enough. You can use the open-source OpenTimestamps tools to verify that the hash existed before the Bitcoin block it was anchored into.

Can I roll this out to clients or a team?

Yes. The current UI works fine as a “lightweight proof terminal” for small teams and client work. Longer-term, you can treat this as a reference design for a deeper integration.
